2. LITERATURE REVIEW — Meta-Analytic Consensus
================================================================================

This section grounds every recommendation in the GENERAL SCIENTIFIC CONSENSUS,
not individual studies. Where meta-analyses exist, they are cited. Individual
studies are cited only when no meta-analysis covers the topic, and this is
explicitly noted.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 What Predicts Initial Attraction
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SIMILARITY-ATTRACTION EFFECT:

    Meta-analysis: Montoya, Horton, & Kirchner (2008)
    460 effect sizes from 313 independent studies

    Key findings:
    - Actual similarity → attraction: r = 0.47 (large effect)
    - Perceived similarity → attraction: r = 0.39 (large effect)

    CRITICAL NUANCE (often missed):
    - Before interaction: actual similarity is strong (r = 0.59)
    - During short interaction: weakens dramatically (r = 0.21)
    - In existing relationships: non-significant (r = 0.08)

    Interpretation: Similarity helps form the INITIAL basis for comfort
    and approach behavior, but once people are interacting face-to-face,
    other factors dominate. For a group date app, similarity provides a
    reasonable BASELINE for group composition, but it is NOT the driver
    of conversion. After the date starts, the interaction itself takes over.

PHYSICAL ATTRACTIVENESS MATCHING:

    Meta-analysis: Feingold (1988)
    Multiple studies on established couples

    Key finding:
    - Partners' attractiveness ratings correlate at r = 0.49

    This is one of the strongest findings in relationship science.
    People pair with others at similar attractiveness levels, not because
    of a conscious preference, but as an equilibrium outcome of mutual
    selection in a competitive market.

    For Yuni: Groups with LOW attractiveness variance will produce more
    mutual interest because everyone feels "in the same league." This is
    NOT about being shallow — it's about removing the psychological
    barrier of feeling outclassed or settling.

STATED vs. REVEALED PREFERENCES:

    Eastwick & Finkel (2008) + Eastwick et al. (2014) replications
    Multiple speed-dating studies with combined N > 10,000

    Consensus finding:
    - What people SAY they want (surveys) does NOT predict who they
      are ACTUALLY attracted to in face-to-face encounters
    - Men claim to value attractiveness more; women claim to value
      resources/ambition more. In person, BOTH sexes weight
      attractiveness equally, and stated preferences for other traits
      predict nothing about actual choices

    Hitsch, Hortaçsu & Ariely (2010) confirmed at scale with online
    dating behavioral data: stated preferences diverge from revealed
    behavior due to social desirability bias, lack of introspective
    access, and context-dependent preferences.

    IMPLICATION FOR YUNI:
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  DO NOT use stated preferences (preferred body type, height,    │
    │  humor style, communication style) as matching inputs.          │
    │  They are scientifically unreliable predictors of actual        │
    │  attraction. Use BEHAVIORAL data from past dates instead.       │
    │                                                                  │
    │  The ONLY stated preferences worth respecting are:              │
    │  - Dealbreakers (genuine hard limits backed by values)          │
    │  - Age range (biological/life-stage relevance)                  │
    │  - Location (logistical necessity)                              │
    │  - Relationship intent (mismatched intents waste everyone's time)│
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

2.2 The Unpredictability of Specific Chemistry
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Joel, Eastwick, & Finkel (2017)
    Psychological Science, 28(10), 1478-1489
    43 speed-dating studies, 100+ variables, machine learning (random forests)

    ┌─────────────────────────────┬──────────────┐
    │ Variance Component          │ R² Explained │
    ├─────────────────────────────┼──────────────┤
    │ Actor effects               │ 4-18%        │
    │ (how desirable YOU are      │              │
    │  to people in general)      │              │
    │                             │              │
    │ Partner effects             │ 7-27%        │
    │ (how desirable THEY are     │              │
    │  to people in general)      │              │
    │                             │              │
    │ Relationship effects        │ ~0%          │
    │ (do YOU + THEY specifically │              │
    │  click with each other?)    │              │
    └─────────────────────────────┴──────────────┘

This is the single most cited finding in modern relationship science, and it
has been REPLICATED and EXTENDED by multiple independent groups (Tidwell et al.,
2013; Finkel et al., 2015; Eastwick et al., 2017).

WHAT THE CONSENSUS SAYS:

    1. GENERAL DESIRABILITY IS REAL AND PREDICTABLE.
       Some people are more attractive, more charismatic, more socially
       skilled. The algorithm CAN identify who is generally popular.
       → This validates Elo-based attractiveness scoring.

    2. SPECIFIC COMPATIBILITY IS NOT PREDICTABLE.
       No combination of personality traits, interests, values, or
       demographics can predict which specific two people will feel
       a mutual spark. The spark is an emergent property of the
       interaction itself.
       → This INVALIDATES pairwise compatibility scoring.

    3. THE INTERACTION IS WHAT MATTERS.
       Chemistry arises from the dynamic, real-time interplay between
       two people — eye contact, humor, conversational rhythm, physical
       synchrony. These cannot be predicted in advance; they can only
       be FACILITATED by creating the right conditions.
       → This is why Yuni must optimize CONDITIONS, not MATCHES.

WHY THIS IS NOT JUST ONE STUDY:

Joel et al. (2017) is the definitive paper, but the finding is corroborated
by the broader body of speed-dating research:

    - Eastwick & Finkel (2008): Stated preferences don't predict choices
    - Tidwell et al. (2013): Perceived, not actual, similarity predicts
    - Todd et al. (2007): Different cognitive processes for preferences
      vs. actual choices
    - Finkel et al. (2015): Comprehensive review of initial romantic
      encounters supporting the unpredictability thesis

This is the scientific consensus, not an outlier finding.

2.3 What Predicts Second-Date Desire (Distinct From Initial Attraction)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SECTION FOR YUNI because our conversion metric
is the second date, not initial attraction.

Initial attraction (the "spark" during the group date) is necessary but NOT
SUFFICIENT. The question is: what makes someone want to see a specific
person AGAIN after the date is over?

EYE CONTACT AND NONVERBAL SYNCHRONY:

    Prochazkova et al. (2023), Archives of Sexual Behavior
    Speed-dating study with physiological measurement

    - Mutual eye contact during interaction: 270% increase in desire
      for second date (vs. 33% increase from perceived attractiveness)
    - Body sway coupling (unconscious physical synchrony) predicted
      romantic interest ABOVE AND BEYOND physical attractiveness

    Allegrini et al. (2021), PMC: Body Sway Predicts Romantic Interest
    - Directional body sway (not just similarity) during conversation
      predicted long-term interest above physical attractiveness

    This means: the QUALITY OF INTERACTION during the date is a far
    stronger predictor of conversion than any pre-date profile data.
    Eye contact and physical synchrony happen naturally in face-to-face
    settings — especially during engaging shared activities.

COMPATIBILITY IMPRESSIONS FORMED DURING INTERACTION:

    Eastwick, Finkel, & Simpson (2019), PNAS
    Three speed-dating studies

    - Both "consensually desirable traits" (general attractiveness) AND
      "unique impressions of compatibility" predicted romantic interest
    - Unique compatibility impressions predicted actual dating behavior
      BETTER than pre-measured trait similarity
    - These impressions are formed DURING interaction and cannot be
      predicted in advance

    This reinforces: you cannot pre-compute compatibility. You can only
    create environments where compatibility impressions form well.

WHAT WOMEN SPECIFICALLY VALUE FOR SECOND DATES:

    Fisman, Iyengar et al. (2006, 2008), Columbia/Stanford speed-dating
    Large-N studies with actual behavioral outcomes

    Women's second-date decisions driven by:
    - Intelligence: 24% weight (highest for women)
    - Attractiveness, sincerity, fun, ambition: weighted more evenly
    - "Clicking" — a reported sense of genuine connection during
      conversation (the strongest qualitative predictor)

    Dealbreakers for women (51.75% cite this):
    - Disrespectful or rude behavior

    Conversational factors that drive second dates:
    - Appreciative language ("That's awesome," "Good for you")
    - Sympathy and validation
    - Men who show engagement by building on stories
    - Perceived humor and wit in real-time (not self-reported humor style)

    IMPLICATION: The group date must create conditions where intelligence,
    sincerity, fun, and conversational engagement are VISIBLE. Passive
    activities (dinner, sitting at a bar) are poor at surfacing these.
    Active, challenging activities (escape rooms, trivia, cooking) are
    good at surfacing them.

2.4 The Match-to-Date Gap: Where 90% of Value Dies
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The dating app industry's dirty secret: matches are abundant, dates are rare.

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │ THE FUNNEL OF DEATH (typical swipe app):                         │
    │                                                                   │
    │ 1,000 right swipes                                               │
    │   → 50 matches (5% match rate for men, higher for women)         │
    │     → 15 conversations started (30% of matches)                  │
    │       → 5 conversations sustained past 5 messages (33%)          │
    │         → 2 phone numbers exchanged                              │
    │           → 1 actual date (if lucky)                             │
    │             → ??? second date                                    │
    │                                                                   │
    │ Conversion: 0.1% from swipe to date.                             │
    │ Most users never go on a single date from the app.               │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

WHY MATCHES DON'T CONVERT TO DATES:

    1. VOLUME OVERWHELM (especially for women):
       Women receive dramatically more matches than they can process.
       Women respond to only 1 in 8 messages. The sorting problem
       exceeds human cognitive capacity.

    2. CONVERSATION FATIGUE:
       70% of Bumble matches stall before plans are made.
       Conversations typically die after 2-5 messages.
       The "talking stage" is where connections go to die.

    3. STRANGER ANXIETY:
       Meeting a stranger from the internet 1-on-1 is inherently
       anxiety-inducing, especially for women. This is a genuine
       psychological barrier, not laziness.

    4. BOREDOM AND DISPOSABILITY:
       Swipe culture makes connections feel disposable. If the
       conversation isn't immediately exciting, the user moves on.
       There's always another match waiting.

    5. GHOSTING EPIDEMIC:
       62% of active dating app users have been ghosted.
       42% of young adults (18-29) report being ghosted.
       Ghosting is normalized and often unintentional — app design
       makes it frictionless to simply stop responding.

SPEED DATING — THE CLOSEST ANALOG TO YUNI:

    Asendorpf, Penke, & Back (2011)
    Large-scale speed-dating longitudinal study

    - 60-87% of participants get at least one mutual match
    - Average: 1.7-2.4 mutual matches per event
    - BUT: Only 6% actually go on a date afterward
    - Only 4% begin any form of relationship

    The gap between "I'd like to see them again" (said at the event)
    and actually meeting again is enormous. 94% attrition.

WHY YUNI BYPASSES THIS:

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  YUNI'S STRUCTURAL ADVANTAGE:                                    │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Speed dating:  Meet for 5 min → Say "interested" → Exchange    │
    │                 contact → ... → 94% never follow through         │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Yuni:          Meet for 2-3 hours of shared activity →          │
    │                 Say "interested" → Chat auto-opens in app →      │
    │                 Already have shared memories/inside jokes →       │
    │                 Much lower barrier to "let's do this again"      │
    │                                                                  │
    │  The 2-3 hours of shared experience is the critical difference.  │
    │  It creates the emotional foundation + conversational momentum   │
    │  that the talking stage is supposed to (but never does) create.  │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    But even with this advantage, we must actively BRIDGE the gap from
    mutual interest to actual second date. This is Section 7.

2.5 Group Dynamics and Attraction
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

GROUP SIZE EFFECTS:

    Dunbar (2010), "How Many Friends Does One Person Need?"
    Decades of research on social brain hypothesis

    Social group size hierarchy: 5, 15, 50, 150, 500
    - 4-5 people: Cognitive sweet spot for direct conversation
    - Everyone can maintain awareness of all relationships in the group
    - Conversation naturally includes all members
    - Self-disclosure is higher than in larger groups

    Solano & Dunnam (1985): Social Psychology Quarterly
    - People disclose MORE in dyads than larger groups
    - As group size increases, willingness to self-disclose decreases
    - Women are MORE INHIBITED in larger groups
    - This means: groups of 6 actively suppress the disclosure that
      builds romantic connection, especially for women

SOCIAL PROOF:

    Meta-analysis: Dimant (2019), Nature Human Behavior
    89 studies, n = 85,759

    - Social proof effect exists but is SMALL: Cohen's d = 0.1
    - Works best in early perception formation (before own opinion forms)
    - In dating context: seeing others positively react to someone
      modestly increases your own attraction to them

    HONEST ASSESSMENT: Social proof is a real but modest effect. It
    should not be a primary design consideration. The group format's
    advantages lie in safety, shared experience, and behavioral
    observation — not in social proof.

SELF-DISCLOSURE AND CLOSENESS:

    Aron et al. (1997): "The experimental generation of interpersonal closeness"
    (Individual study — no meta-analysis available on this specific protocol)

    - Structured reciprocal self-disclosure between strangers generated
      measurable closeness in 45 minutes
    - The process of disclosing creates closeness, not just the content
    - Escalating intimacy (light → personal → vulnerable) builds trust

    IMPLICATION: The pre-date group chat is an underutilized asset.
    Structured escalating prompts before the date could prime the
    disclosure patterns that build closeness during the date.

2.6 Shared Experience and Bonding
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SYNCHRONIZED AFFECT:

    Multiple studies (no single definitive meta-analysis; consensus from
    review papers)

    Participating in shared activities creates social bonds through:
    1. Simultaneous emotional experiences
    2. Physiological synchrony (heart rate, breathing align)
    3. Cognitive alignment (shared reference points, inside jokes)
    4. Behavioral synchrony (mirroring body language)

    A 2024 Royal Society Open Science paper found groups engaging in
    shared activities showed significantly stronger bonding than groups
    doing parallel individual activities.

MISATTRIBUTION OF AROUSAL — DOWNGRADED:

    The famous Dutton & Aron (1974) "shaky bridge" study claimed that
    physiological arousal from fear gets misattributed to romantic
    attraction.

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  REPLICATION STATUS: MIXED / WEAKENED                            │
    │                                                                  │
    │  - Subsequent replications produced inconsistent results          │
    │  - The original study NEVER MEASURED physiological arousal in     │
    │    participants — they assumed the bridge caused arousal but      │
    │    didn't verify it                                               │
    │  - Recent reanalysis found "methodological, theoretical, and     │
    │    interpretative incoherences" (Polish Psychological Bulletin)   │
    │  - Effect is real in controlled lab settings but less robust      │
    │    and reliable than commonly portrayed                           │
    │                                                                  │
    │  HONEST ASSESSMENT: Do NOT design around arousal misattribution. │
    │  Instead, design around the RELIABLE mechanism: shared positive   │
    │  experience creates genuine bonding. Exciting activities work     │
    │  because they're FUN and CREATE MEMORIES, not because of a       │
    │  questionable arousal transfer effect.                            │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

WHAT ACTUALLY MATTERS ABOUT ACTIVITIES:

Activities that produce high conversion will be those that:
    1. Create SHARED MEMORIES (something to reference later)
    2. Surface PERSONALITY (intelligence, humor, kindness become visible)
    3. Require COLLABORATION (builds rapport and compatibility impressions)
    4. Enable CONVERSATION (not too loud/passive; natural breaks for talking)
    5. Generate POSITIVE EMOTION (fun, laughter, surprise, accomplishment)

    Escape rooms: ★★★★★ (collaboration, personality surfacing, shared achievement)
    Cooking class: ★★★★☆ (collaboration, sensory experience, conversation)
    Trivia night: ★★★★☆ (intelligence display, team bonding, fun)
    Hiking: ★★★★☆ (natural conversation, shared adventure, no phones)
    Karaoke: ★★★☆☆ (vulnerability, fun, but limits conversation)
    Bowling/mini golf: ★★★☆☆ (playful, turn-taking enables side conversations)
    Dinner: ★★☆☆☆ (conversation-heavy but can feel like an interview)
    Bar/drinks: ★★☆☆☆ (low structure, relies entirely on conversation skill)
    Art gallery: ★☆☆☆☆ (passive, limited interaction structure)

2.7 The Paradox of Choice in Dating
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Schwartz (2004), "The Paradox of Choice"
    Supported by subsequent research on choice overload in dating contexts

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  With abundance of options: only 17% find romantic partnerships │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Users with multiple dating apps open report LOWER commitment    │
    │  and relationship satisfaction than those who delete apps.        │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Mere perceptual availability of alternatives erodes investment  │
    │  in the present partner.                                         │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    The insight: MODERATE SCARCITY + STRUCTURAL COMMITMENT works better
    than either extreme (no options or infinite options).

    YUNI'S MODEL IS WELL-DESIGNED HERE:
    - 1 free date per week (moderate scarcity)
    - Groups of 4: only 2 cross-gender options (focused, not overwhelming)
    - Must commit to a date/time/activity (structural commitment)
    - Can't endlessly browse profiles (no swipe mechanism)

    These are features, not limitations. The paradox of choice research
    says Yuni's constrained model should produce BETTER outcomes than
    unlimited-swipe apps. This is a marketable insight.

2.8 Gender Dynamics in Dating Markets
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

OPERATIONAL SEX RATIO (OSR) RESEARCH:

    Large body of research across evolutionary biology, sociology, economics
    (Trivers 1972; Guttentag & Secord 1983; Pedersen 1991; numerous modern studies)

    When men outnumber women (male-biased OSR — typical in dating apps):
    - Women become CHOOSIER and more selective
    - Men become more competitive and aggressive
    - Women have greater bargaining power
    - Overall: fewer relationships form, but those that do are higher quality

    Dating app gender ratios:
    - Tinder: ~75% male
    - Bumble: ~65% male
    - Hinge: ~64% male
    - The average dating app is 60-80% male

    CRITICAL IMPLICATION:
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Gender ratio is not a "nice to have" metric.                    │
    │  It is THE fundamental driver of market dynamics.                │
    │                                                                  │
    │  If Yuni is 70% male:                                           │
    │  - Women are overwhelmed with attention → burnout → leave        │
    │  - Men can't get matched → frustration → leave                  │
    │  - Death spiral                                                  │
    │                                                                  │
    │  If Yuni is 50/50:                                               │
    │  - Women feel valued, not overwhelmed                            │
    │  - Men get matched reliably                                      │
    │  - Both sides have good experience → retention → growth          │
    │                                                                  │
    │  ACHIEVING 50/50 IS A CORE PRODUCT REQUIREMENT,                 │
    │  NOT A MARKETING AFTERTHOUGHT.                                   │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

WHY WOMEN LEAVE DATING APPS:

    Aggregated from multiple large surveys (Pew 2023, Cloudwards 2025,
    Forbes Health 2024, YouGov 2024):

    1. SAFETY/HARASSMENT (45% cite this): Unsolicited sexual messages,
       stalking concerns, fear of meeting strangers alone
    2. BURNOUT (78% of Gen Z): Feeling drained, viewing dating as a chore,
       swiping feels like work
    3. COMMODIFICATION: Being reduced to photos, judged on appearance,
       treated as disposable
    4. GHOSTING/LOW EFFORT: Matches who never message, conversations that
       go nowhere, feeling like they're always the one making effort
    5. MENTAL HEALTH: Anxiety, decreased self-esteem, comparison fatigue

4. THE CONVERSION OPTIMIZATION FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 Formal Problem Definition
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let U = {u₁, u₂, ..., uₙ} be users in a matching batch.
Let π = {G₁, G₂, ..., Gₖ} be a group assignment.

CONVERSION RATE:

    C = (1/|assigned users|) × Σⱼ SecondDates(Gⱼ)

    Where SecondDates(Gⱼ) = number of users in Gⱼ who meet someone
    from Gⱼ again with romantic intent.

We cannot directly optimize C because SecondDates is only observable
weeks after the group date. We need PROXY objectives.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.2 Conversion Decomposition
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    C = P(second_date | attended group date)

    Decompose:

    C = P(mutual_interest) × P(second_date | mutual_interest)

    TERM 1: P(mutual_interest)
    The probability that at least one cross-gender pair in the group
    mutually expresses romantic interest.

    Factors that increase P(mutual_interest):
    (a) Attractiveness cohesion (matching hypothesis: r = 0.49)
    (b) Group dynamics quality (everyone feels comfortable, included)
    (c) Activity-personality fit (the activity surfaces attractive traits)
    (d) Personality diversity (more different "types" → higher P someone
        appeals to any given individual)
    (e) Absence of dealbreaker friction
    (f) Relationship intent alignment

    TERM 2: P(second_date | mutual_interest)
    The probability that a mutual match actually meets again.

    Factors that increase P(second_date | mutual_interest):
    (a) Strength of interaction quality (eye contact, synchrony: 270%)
    (b) Shared memories from the date (conversational anchors)
    (c) Low friction path to second date (facilitated scheduling)
    (d) Momentum preservation (no long gap between match and action)
    (e) Continued engagement (post-date chat activity)

    THE KEY INSIGHT:
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Most dating apps only optimize Term 1 (mutual interest).       │
    │  They completely ignore Term 2 (follow-through).                │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Speed dating shows that Term 2 is where 94% of value is lost.  │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Yuni must optimize BOTH terms:                                  │
    │  - The algorithm + group design optimizes Term 1                 │
    │  - The "Second-Date Bridge" (Section 7) optimizes Term 2        │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.4 Mathematical Formulation for Group Optimization
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We optimize a GROUP-LEVEL quality function Q(G) that serves as a proxy
for P(mutual_interest | G):

    maximize  Σⱼ Q(Gⱼ)

    Subject to HARD CONSTRAINTS:
    (C1) |Gⱼ| = 4 (default) or 6 (when pre-grouping requires)
    (C2) |{u ∈ Gⱼ : gender(u) = M}| = |Gⱼ|/2       (gender balance)
    (C3) ∀u,v ∈ Gⱼ: v ∉ Blocked(u)                   (no blocked pairs)
    (C4) ∀u,v ∈ Gⱼ: Dealbreaker(u,v) = 0              (no dealbreaker violations)
    (C5) ∩{Availability(u) : u ∈ Gⱼ} ≠ ∅              (time overlap)
    (C6) ∩{Activities(u) : u ∈ Gⱼ} ≠ ∅                (activity overlap)
    (C7) ∀u ∈ Gⱼ, ∀v ∈ PreGroup(u): v ∈ Gⱼ           (friends together)
    (C8) ∀u,v ∈ Gⱼ: age(u) ∈ [age_min(v), age_max(v)] (age compatibility)
    (C9) ∀u,v ∈ Gⱼ: distance(u,v) ≤ max_distance(u,v) (location)

    GROUP-LEVEL QUALITY FUNCTION (see Section 6 for details):

    Q(Gⱼ) = w₁ × AttCohesion(Gⱼ)      [attractiveness band tightness]
           + w₂ × RoleDiversity(Gⱼ)     [conversation catalyst present]
           + w₃ × EnergyBalance(Gⱼ)     [moderate energy diversity]
           + w₄ × PersonalityDiv(Gⱼ)    [maximize type diversity]
           + w₅ × IntentAlignment(Gⱼ)   [relationship intent match]
           + w₆ × ActivityFit(Gⱼ, act)  [activity-energy alignment]
           + w₇ × ValuesBaseline(Gⱼ)    [moderate values similarity]
           - w₈ × FrictionScore(Gⱼ)     [lifestyle/logistic friction]

    All weights are LEARNABLE and updated from outcome data.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.5 Computational Complexity and Practical Solutions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The group formation problem is NP-hard (variant of balanced k-way partitioning).

    For N users forming groups of 4:
    - Candidate groups: C(N/2, 2)² (choose 2 males × 2 females)
    - For N=100: ~1.5 million candidates
    - For N=500: ~1.5 billion candidates

    PRACTICAL ALGORITHM:
    1. Pre-filter: Apply hard constraints to reduce candidate space
    2. Activity clustering: Separate users by activity preference
    3. Availability clustering: Within activity, cluster by time overlap
    4. Greedy assignment: Score all valid groups, assign highest first
    5. Local search: 2-opt swaps between assigned groups
    6. Random restarts: 20 iterations with shuffled candidate order
    7. Return best overall assignment

    Runtime: < 5 seconds for N=500 on modern hardware.

    For larger pools (N > 1000): Simulated annealing or genetic algorithm
    with population size 100, 500 generations.

5.3 What Was Removed and Why
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    ✗ ATTACHMENT STYLE: Predicts relationship satisfaction 6 months in,
      not second-date desire. Effect size on initial attraction: ~0.
      (Hazan & Shaver research is about long-term outcomes.)
      Wasted onboarding time for our metric.

    ✗ EMOTIONAL STABILITY / BIG FIVE: The r=0.29 correlation with
      relationship satisfaction is an ACTOR effect (your own neuroticism
      affects YOUR satisfaction). It does not predict whether a specific
      pair will feel mutual attraction. Joel et al.: 0% relationship R².
      Does not predict conversion.

    ✗ HUMOR STYLES: Self-reported humor style is unreliable. What matters
      is DISPLAYED humor during the date — which you see in person, not in
      a survey. The group activity is designed to surface actual humor.

    ✗ COMMUNICATION PREFERENCE: Relevant AFTER you start dating, not for
      group formation or initial attraction prediction.

    ✗ BODY TYPE / HEIGHT / STYLE (self + preferences): Eastwick & Finkel
      (2008) across multiple studies: stated physical preferences do NOT
      predict who people are actually attracted to in person. Photos already
      capture physical appearance. Preference questions add noise, not signal.

    ✗ EXERCISE / SLEEP SCHEDULE: Zero evidence these predict anything
      relevant to dating outcomes at any timescale.

    ✗ PROGRAM / YEAR OF STUDY: No predictive value beyond age (already
      captured). Keep as optional profile display.

    ✗ QUICK/THOROUGH SPLIT: Creates a two-class system where quick-path
      users get worse matching. One optimized path for everyone.

5.4 The Cold Start Problem
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

New users have zero behavioral data. Strategy:

    DATE 1 (zero history):
    - Attractiveness: ML photo score (prior = population mean 5.0)
    - Desirability: Elo = 1000 (default)
    - Matching: Based entirely on onboarding data + hard constraints
    - Confidence: LOW → algorithm explores more (see Section 6.6)

    DATE 2-3 (sparse history):
    - Attractiveness: Bayesian blend of ML + first Elo updates
    - Behavioral signal: who did they express interest in?
    - Matching: Onboarding + early behavioral data

    DATE 4+ (sufficient history):
    - Elo dominates ML photo score
    - Behavioral patterns visible (what "type" do they pick?)
    - Collaborative filtering begins to apply

    FORMULA:
    attractiveness_score = α × Elo + (1-α) × ML_photo
    Where α = min(1.0, n_dates_completed / 5)

    After 5 dates, Elo fully dominates. Elo captures charisma, humor,
    presence — everything that photos miss.

    ╚═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.2 Hard Constraint Filtering
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    These are binary pass/fail. A group violating ANY hard constraint
    is discarded:

    (C1) Group size: 4 (default) or 6 (only when pre-grouping requires)
    (C2) Gender balance: exactly 2M/2F (or 3M/3F for groups of 6)
    (C3) No blocked pairs
    (C4) No dealbreaker violations
    (C5) Time availability overlap
    (C6) Activity preference overlap
    (C7) Pre-grouped friends stay together
    (C8) Bidirectional age range compatibility
    (C9) Location within max distance for all members

    No changes from current implementation. These are correct.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.3 Attractiveness Band Clustering
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Supported by: Matching Hypothesis (Feingold 1988, r = 0.49)

    AttCohesion(G) = -σ²(attractiveness scores in G)

    Lower variance = better group.

    Attractiveness score computation:
    score = α × Elo + (1-α) × ML_photo
    Where α = min(1.0, n_dates / 5)

    Elo update after each date:
    For each cross-gender member j in user i's group:
        Expected = 1 / (1 + 10^((Elo_j - Elo_i) / 400))
        Actual = interest_level_received (0, 0.25, 0.75, 1.0)
        Elo_new = Elo + K × (Actual - Expected)
    K = 32 for first 10 dates, K = 16 thereafter

    Soft band clustering:
    Band 1: [1, 3)   Band 2: [3, 5)   Band 3: [5, 7)
    Band 4: [7, 9)   Band 5: [9, 10]
    Users near boundaries can be in either adjacent band.

    INITIAL WEIGHT: w₁ = 5.0 (highest weight — strongest evidence)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.4 Group Chemistry Scoring
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    RoleDiversity(G):
        roles = {role(u) for u in G}
        score = |roles| / |G|  (proportion of unique roles)
        bonus = 3.0 if group contains at least one Catalyst
        return score × 5 + bonus

        WHY CATALYST MATTERS: A group without someone who initiates
        conversation risks awkward silences. Awkward silences are the
        #1 killer of group chemistry. One catalyst person transforms
        the entire dynamic.

    EnergyBalance(G):
        energies = [energy(u) for u in G]
        std_e = std(energies)
        # Want moderate diversity — not all introverts, not all extroverts
        return 3.0 - |std_e - 1.0| × 2  (optimal std ≈ 1.0)

    PersonalityDiversity(G):
        # Maximize the variety of "types" in the group
        # This maximizes P(at least one person appeals to each user)
        # Because chemistry is unpredictable, diversity hedges our bets
        vectors = [attribute_vector(u) for u in G]  # values + energy + role
        return entropy(vectors)  # normalized to [0, 5]

        THIS IS THE KEY CONVERSION LEVER: If everyone in the group is the
        same "type," a user who doesn't like that type has zero options.
        If the group has diverse types, each user is more likely to find
        at least one person intriguing.

    IntentAlignment(G):
        intents = [intent(u) for u in G]
        if all_same(intents): return 3.0
        if 'open' in intents: return 1.5
        return 0.0  (serious + casual clash)

    ValuesBaseline(G):
        # Cross-gender Hamming distance on 6-bit value vectors
        distances = [hamming(v_i, v_j) for cross-gender pairs]
        avg_dist = mean(distances)
        # Optimal: moderate similarity (~2-3 out of 6 match)
        # Too similar = boring; too different = friction
        return 3.0 - |avg_dist - 2.5| × 1.5

    FrictionScore(G):
        friction = 0
        if activity is dining-related:
            diets = [diet(u) for u in G if diet(u)]
            if 'vegan' in diets and 'no_restrictions' in diets:
                friction += 1.0
        return friction

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.5 Activity-Group Fit
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    ActivityFit(G, activity):
        ideal_energy = {
            "escape_room": 3.5,  "karaoke": 4.0,   "hiking": 3.0,
            "cooking_class": 2.5, "dinner": 2.5,    "bar": 3.5,
            "bowling": 3.0,      "board_games": 2.0, "trivia": 3.0,
            "mini_golf": 3.0,    "art_gallery": 2.0, "concert": 4.0,
        }
        group_energy = mean([energy(u) for u in G])
        return max(0, 3.0 - |group_energy - ideal_energy[activity]|)

    THESE VALUES ARE INITIAL PRIORS. They will be updated from actual
    conversion data by activity type × group energy level. See Section 6.7.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.6 Exploration-Exploitation via Epsilon-Greedy
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    The current algorithm is purely exploitative — always picks the
    highest-scoring group. This means it can never discover that its
    weights are wrong.

    EPSILON-GREEDY:
    - With probability ε: form groups using random valid assignments
    - With probability (1-ε): form groups using best-scoring assignments

    Schedule: ε = max(0.05, 0.20 - 0.025 × month)
    Start at 20% random, decay to 5% over 6 months.

    Random groups provide unbiased outcome data for learning.
    Exploit groups maximize conversion for current users.

    UPGRADE PATH: When data science infrastructure supports it, move to
    Thompson Sampling (sample weights from posterior distributions).
    This is more sample-efficient but harder to implement.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.7 The Learning Loop
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    After each batch of dates completes and feedback is collected:

    1. COMPUTE REWARD for each group (see Section 7.5)
    2. EXTRACT FEATURES for each group (all scoring components)
    3. RUN REGRESSION: Reward ~ w₁×Feature₁ + ... + w₈×Feature₈
    4. UPDATE WEIGHTS: Use regression coefficients as new weights
       (with regularization toward current weights for stability)
    5. UPDATE ACTIVITY ENERGY MAP: Compute conversion by
       (activity, mean_group_energy) buckets

    Frequency: Weekly batch updates (accumulate enough data points)

    COLLABORATIVE FILTERING (Phase 6, requires 500+ users):
    - Build user × user interest matrix R
    - Matrix factorization: R ≈ U × V^T (d=20 latent dimensions)
    - P(i likes j) ≈ σ(Uᵢ · Vⱼ^T)
    - P(mutual) ≈ σ(Uᵢ·Vⱼ) × σ(Uⱼ·Vᵢ)
    - Incorporate as additional scoring component, capped at 30% weight
    - Prevents filter bubbles: never let CF dominate condition optimization

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.8 Complete Scoring Formula
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Q(G) = w₁ × AttCohesion(G)         Initial: w₁ = 5.0
         + w₂ × RoleDiversity(G)        Initial: w₂ = 3.0
         + w₃ × EnergyBalance(G)        Initial: w₃ = 1.5
         + w₄ × PersonalityDiv(G)       Initial: w₄ = 2.5
         + w₅ × IntentAlignment(G)      Initial: w₅ = 2.0
         + w₆ × ActivityFit(G, act)     Initial: w₆ = 1.5
         + w₇ × ValuesBaseline(G)       Initial: w₇ = 1.5
         - w₈ × FrictionScore(G)        Initial: w₈ = 1.5
         + β × CFScore(G)               Initial: β = 0.0

    ALL WEIGHTS ARE INITIAL PRIORS. Updated weekly from outcome data.
    β grows with data: β = min(0.3, n_date_events / 10000)

    NOTE: There is NO pairwise compatibility score in this formula.
    Every component is a GROUP-LEVEL property. This is the paradigm shift.

This is Yuni's most important innovation opportunity. No other dating app

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.1 Where Connections Die (and How to Save Them)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    CURRENT YUNI FLOW:
    Mutual match → 1-on-1 chat opens → ... → ???

    The "..." is where connections die. Industry data:
    - 70% of app conversations die after 2-5 messages
    - Conversation quality degrades rapidly after initial excitement
    - The longer the gap between matching and meeting, the less likely
      they'll ever meet

    THE PROBLEM IS NOT MATCHING. IT IS FOLLOW-THROUGH.

    95% of people who plan to meet do so within one week.
    After one week, the probability of meeting drops to near zero.

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  THE SECOND-DATE BRIDGE:                                         │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Don't just open a chat. Actively facilitate the second date.    │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Within 48 hours of mutual match:                                │
    │  1. Auto-suggest a 1-on-1 date idea                             │
    │     "You both loved [escape room]. Try [related activity]?"      │
    │  2. Show mutual availability overlap                             │
    │     "You're both free Saturday afternoon"                        │
    │  3. Suggest a venue                                              │
    │     "[Venue name] is between both of you, 4.5★ rated"           │
    │  4. One-tap "Propose this date" button                          │
    │     Partner gets notification: "[Name] wants to [activity] at    │
    │     [venue] on [day]. Accept?"                                   │
    │                                                                  │
    │  Make the second date as frictionless as the first group date.   │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    This is not pushy. It's helpful. The user already said they're
    interested. The app is just removing the friction that kills 94%
    of speed-dating matches.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.2 Expanded Post-Date Feedback
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    SECTION 1: GROUP EXPERIENCE (20 sec)

    Q1: "How was the overall experience?" (1-5 stars)
    Q2: "How well did the group gel?" (1-5: Awkward ↔ Amazing)
    Q3: "Was [Activity] a good fit?" (1-5)

    SECTION 2: INDIVIDUAL IMPRESSIONS (30 sec)

    For each cross-gender group member:

    Q4: "How interested are you in [Name]?"
        (a) Not interested
        (b) Maybe — would need to see them again
        (c) Interested — would like to connect
        (d) Very interested — definitely want to see them again

    MATCHING LOGIC:
    • Both "Interested" or "Very interested" → FULL MATCH (chat opens)
    • One "Interested/Very" + one "Maybe" → SOFT MATCH (see 7.3)
    • Both "Maybe" → No match (but not blocked)
    • Any "Not interested" → No match

    Q5: "Would you hang out with [Name] as friends?" (Yes / No)
        Separates social compatibility from romantic interest.

    SECTION 3: QUICK REFLECTION (15 sec, optional)

    Q6: "What made this date great (or not)?" (select all that apply)
        ✓ Good conversation flow
        ✓ Fun activity
        ✓ Felt comfortable and included
        ✓ Someone really caught my eye
        ✗ Awkward silences
        ✗ Activity wasn't my thing
        ✗ Felt left out
        ✗ Someone dominated the conversation

    Block and Report options remain unchanged.

    WOMEN SUBMIT FIRST:
    Women's feedback is collected first. Men's feedback is held until
    all women in the group have submitted. This gives women the
    psychological safety of knowing their response is independent.

    TOTAL FEEDBACK TIME: ~65 seconds

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.3 The Soft Match Mechanism
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    When one person says "Interested" and the other says "Maybe":

    CURRENT BEHAVIOR: No match. Connection lost forever.

    PROPOSED: SOFT MATCH

    48 hours after the date, the "Maybe" person gets a notification:

    "Someone from your [Activity] group is interested in seeing you again.
     Want to find out who? 💫"

    If they tap yes:
    - Match is revealed
    - Chat opens
    - Second-date suggestion appears (same as full matches)

    If they don't tap or tap no:
    - Nothing happens
    - The interested person is never notified of the "maybe"
    - No embarrassment for either party

    WHY THIS MATTERS:
    - "Maybe" often means "I'm not sure, I'd need to see them again"
    - In a group date, you have limited 1-on-1 time with each person
    - A soft match gives a second chance for borderline connections
    - This could recover 10-20% of connections that would otherwise be lost
    - The delay (48 hours) allows for "slow burn" attraction to develop
      (some people become more attractive in memory than they seemed in
      the moment)

7.5 Reward Signal Design
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    The reward signal trains the matching algorithm. It must be
    weighted toward our conversion metric: SECOND DATE.

    REWARD COMPONENTS:

    IMMEDIATE (within 24 hours):
    r₁ = n_romantic_interests / possible_pairs      weight: 0.20
    r₂ = n_mutual_matches / possible_pairs           weight: 0.25
    r₃ = group_chemistry_rating / 5                  weight: 0.10
    r₄ = activity_fit_rating / 5                     weight: 0.05

    SHORT-TERM (within 1 week):
    r₅ = any_1on1_message_sent                       weight: 0.05
    r₆ = second_date_proposed (binary)               weight: 0.10

    CONVERSION SIGNAL (within 2 weeks):
    r₇ = second_date_confirmed (binary)              weight: 0.15
    r₈ = follow_up_positive ("met up" or "good vibes") weight: 0.10

    COMPOSITE REWARD:
    R = 0.20×r₁ + 0.25×r₂ + 0.10×r₃ + 0.05×r₄
      + 0.05×r₅ + 0.10×r₆ + 0.15×r₇ + 0.10×r₈

    60% of weight is on immediate/short-term signals (available fast,
    used for rapid algorithm updates). 25% is on actual second-date
    conversion (the real metric, but delayed). 15% is experience quality
    (user retention signal).

    NOTE: No long-term relationship signals (still messaging after months,
    etc.) are included. Those would bias the algorithm toward relationship
    durability, which is our secondary metric, not primary.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.2 Group Size: Default to 4
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    EVIDENCE:
    - Dunbar: 4-5 is cognitive sweet spot for direct conversation
    - Self-disclosure decreases with group size (Solano & Dunnam 1985)
    - Women are MORE INHIBITED in larger groups (female-first concern)
    - Each person gets more conversation time (critical for "clicking")
    - Logistics are simpler (easier to find venues, schedule)

    MATHEMATICAL CONSIDERATION:
    In groups of 4 (2M + 2F), each person has 2 cross-gender options.
    In groups of 6 (3M + 3F), each person has 3 cross-gender options.

    More options sounds better, but:
    - Less time per person → shallower connections
    - Women disclose less → lower "clicking" probability
    - Per-pair match probability likely drops in larger groups

    If p₄ (per-pair match probability in groups of 4) is even 20% higher
    than p₆ (in groups of 6), groups of 4 produce more conversions per user.

    RECOMMENDATION: Default to 4. A/B test against 6. Let data decide.

    Groups of 6 reserved for:
    - Pre-grouped friends (2+1 structure requires 6)
    - Specific high-energy activities (team trivia, sports)
    - User explicit opt-in (rare)

8.3 Activity Intelligence

    TIER 1 — HIGHEST CONVERSION (collaborative, personality-surfacing):
    • Escape rooms (collaboration, shared challenge, celebration)

    TIER 2 — HIGH CONVERSION (active, fun):

    3. Track conversion by activity and update rankings with real data
    4. Seasonal rotation (winter: indoor Tier 1; summer: outdoor Tier 2)
    5. Consider REMOVING Tier 4 activities or warning about lower engagement

8.4 Pre-Date Structured Disclosure
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Based on Aron et al. (1997): structured reciprocal self-disclosure
    generates measurable closeness in 45 minutes.

    REPLACE single icebreaker with ESCALATING PROMPT SERIES:

    Day -3 (group chat opens):
    "Let's break the ice! Everyone share your most controversial
     food opinion. 🔥"

    Day -2:
    "What's something you're weirdly proud of that most people
     don't know about?"

    Day -1:
    "One thing you're looking forward to about tomorrow?"

    Day of (2 hours before):
    "Almost time! Drop your outfit check 💫"

    WHY THIS WORKS:
    - Creates pre-date familiarity (propinquity effect)
    - Establishes reciprocal disclosure patterns
    - Provides conversation anchors for the actual date
    - Builds anticipation and social commitment (reduces no-shows)

    HYPOTHESIS: Groups that engage in pre-date chat will have
    20-30% higher conversion. Track and validate.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.5 Hybrid Format: Optional 1-on-1 Dates
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    The group date is the PRIMARY format and should remain so.
    However, there are scenarios where a 1-on-1 option adds value:

    SCENARIO 1: RE-MATCH DATES
    Two users who were in the same group, BOTH said "Maybe" (no match),
    but the algorithm detects high compatibility signals (similar Elo band,
    overlapping interests, both gave high group ratings). After 2 weeks:

    "You and [Name] were in the same group at [Activity]. Want a second
     chance to get to know each other? We can set up a casual 1-on-1."

    This recovers connections that needed more time — the group date
    gave them a foundation, but 2 hours wasn't enough.

    SCENARIO 2: SECOND-DATE FACILITATION
    All full and soft matches are inherently 1-on-1. The second-date
    bridge (Section 7.4) is already a 1-on-1 date facilitation system.
    Adding formal 1-on-1 date support (venue suggestions, scheduling,
    reminders) is a natural extension.

    SCENARIO 3: "DOUBLE DATE" FORMAT
    Two mutual matches from different groups go on a double date together.
    Each person has their match + one other couple for social support.
    This is a hybrid: safer than pure 1-on-1, more intimate than a full
    group of strangers.

    RECOMMENDATION: Implement Scenario 2 first (it's the second-date bridge).
    A/B test Scenarios 1 and 3 when data permits.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8.6 The Re-Match Mechanism
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Current: Users who don't match in a group date can be re-matched
    in future groups. But there's no mechanism to pair two people who
    were in the same group and BOTH showed tepid interest.

    PROPOSED: After 2 weeks, if two users from the same group both gave
    ratings of "Maybe" or both said "would hang out as friends":

    "Second Look 👀: You and [Name] were in the same [Activity] group.
     Neither of you made a move — but we think there might be something
     there. Want to explore it?"

    If BOTH tap yes → Soft match opens.
    If either says no → Nothing happens, no notification.

    WHY: Many romantic connections start as "not sure." Given more context
    and time to reflect, borderline interest can crystallize. This costs
    nothing and could recover 5-10% additional conversions.

    - Analytics events + group outcomes tables
    - Gender ratio monitoring + alerts
    - A/B experiment framework (merged from old Phase 7)
    - Must be COMPLETE before any feature ships

PHASE 2: ONBOARDING REDESIGN (3 weeks)
    - Single 7-step path, values assessment, remove stated prefs
    - A/B test: values vector ON vs OFF

PHASE 3: FEEDBACK REDESIGN (3 weeks) — MOVED BEFORE matching algorithm
    - 4-point interest scale, soft matches, women-submit-first
    - Produces the SIGNAL the algorithm needs to learn
    - A/B test: 4-point vs binary, soft matches ON vs OFF

PHASE 4: MATCHING ALGORITHM (4 weeks)
    - Group-level Q function, epsilon-greedy, learnable weights
    - Now has feedback signals to learn from (Phase 3)

PHASE 5: GENDER RATIO & GROWTH (2 weeks) — MOVED EARLIER
    - Waitlist, referral, women-confirm-first, pre-date prompts

PHASE 6: SECOND-DATE BRIDGE (3 weeks)
    - Auto-suggest dates, venue recommendation, follow-up, check-in

PHASE 7: WEIGHT LEARNING (2 weeks)
    - Reward signal computation, weekly regression, activity tracking

PHASE 8: COLLABORATIVE FILTERING (4 weeks, deferred until 500+ users)

MVP: Phases 0-3 = 10 weeks. Admin matching covers Phases 4+.
FULL: Phases 0-7 = 25 weeks.
