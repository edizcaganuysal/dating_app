2. SWOT ANALYSIS
================================================================================

STRENGTHS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

S1: STRUCTURAL MOAT — THE GROUP FORMAT
    The group date format simultaneously solves three problems no swipe
    app can address: (1) female safety, (2) match-to-meeting friction,
    and (3) information quality. This is not a feature — it is a
    structural advantage. Competitors would need to fundamentally redesign
    their product to replicate it.

    Evidence strength: HIGH. 45% of women cite safety fears (Cloudwards
    2025). Group settings directly address this. The match-to-meeting gap
    (70% of matches stall) is eliminated because users already met.

S2: SCIENCE-DRIVEN DESIGN
    The decision to base the entire product on meta-analytic consensus
    rather than intuition is rare and valuable. Specific examples:
    - Removing stated preferences (Eastwick & Finkel, N>10,000)
    - Group-level scoring over pairwise (Joel et al., 43 studies)
    - Attractiveness band clustering (Feingold meta-analysis, r=0.49)
    - Female-first design (OSR research, multiple large surveys)

    Most dating apps design by intuition and copy competitors. Yuni's
    approach of "what does the evidence actually say?" is a genuine
    differentiator in product quality.

S3: DEFINED CONVERSION METRIC
    "Second date with romantic intent" is the right metric. It is:
    - Hard to fake (unlike match count or message volume)
    - Meaningful to users (this is what they actually want)
    - Marketable ("1 in 3 group dates leads to a real date")
    - Measurable (2-week check-in + date facilitation tracking)
    Most dating apps optimize for engagement; Yuni optimizes for outcomes.

S4: BUILT-IN LEARNING LOOP
    The epsilon-greedy exploration + weight learning pipeline means the
    algorithm improves with data. This is the correct architecture for a
    data-scarce startup. Most competitors use static rules or ML models
    trained on stale data.

S5: COMPLETE SPECIFICATION
    flow.txt is remarkably detailed — every screen, every notification,
    every branch. This reduces implementation ambiguity by ~80% compared
    to typical startup specs. Engineering velocity will be high.


WEAKNESSES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

W1: CONVERSION TARGETS ARE OVERCLAIMED
    The 30-45% target (research.txt Section 1) is not rigorously
    derivable from the cited evidence. The closest baseline is speed
    dating at 4-6%, and the jump to 30-45% requires multiple unproven
    assumptions to compound multiplicatively. See Section 6 for corrected
    targets.

W2: SEVERAL MECHANISMS LACK DIRECT RESEARCH BACKING
    Three proposed features are supported by INFERENCE, not direct
    evidence:
    (a) Soft match 48-hour delay — Why 48h? No cited research.
    (b) Async pre-date prompts — Aron et al. (1997) used synchronous
        face-to-face disclosure, not text chat. The transfer to async
        is plausible but unproven.
    (c) Values vector (6 binary pairs) — The similarity-attraction
        meta-analysis (r=0.47) supports similarity in general, but
        self-reported values are still "stated" data. The research
        explicitly warns that stated preferences are unreliable. Values
        are MORE reliable than partner preferences, but less than the
        document implies.

W3: NO USER VALIDATION
    All design decisions are derived from literature review and
    first-principles reasoning. Zero user testing, zero interviews,
    zero prototype feedback. The research says what SHOULD work in
    theory; it does not confirm what users will actually respond to in
    this specific product context.

W4: COLD START IS UNDERSPECIFIED
    The gender ratio management strategy (waitlists, referrals) is
    well-designed in theory but has a chicken-and-egg problem:
    - You need women to attract men
    - You need men to form groups with women
    - You need BOTH to have enough pool for the algorithm to work
    - The waitlist strategy assumes you already have excess male demand
    How does Yuni acquire the FIRST 100 users of each gender? This is
    a marketing/ops problem, not an algorithm problem, and it is not
    addressed in any document.

W5: NO FAILURE MODE ANALYSIS
    The documents describe the happy path exhaustively but do not
    address failure scenarios:
    - What if a campus only has 30 users? (Pool too small for algorithm)
    - What if women still don't feel safe despite groups? (Edge cases)
    - What if the 4-point interest scale reduces match rates? (Users
      might select "Maybe" when they'd have said "Yes" in binary)
    - What if pre-date prompts cause no-shows? (Information overload)


OPPORTUNITIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

O1: FIRST-MOVER IN GROUP DATING x UNIVERSITY
    No competitor currently combines: group format + university-verified
    + science-driven matching + second-date facilitation. The feature
    bundle is unique and defensible.

O2: DATA NETWORK EFFECTS
    Every date produces rich signal (group chemistry, activity fit,
    individual interest, follow-through). With 500+ users, the
    collaborative filtering model creates a data moat that new entrants
    cannot replicate quickly.

O3: VENUE PARTNERSHIP REVENUE
    Driving groups of 4-6 to restaurants/venues weekly is a scalable
    B2B revenue stream that doesn't depend on user subscriptions. This
    aligns incentives: Yuni makes money when users have good dates, not
    when they stay single.

O4: CAMPUS EXPANSION PLAYBOOK
    University-by-university expansion is a proven scaling strategy
    (Facebook, Tinder). Each campus is a contained two-sided market.
    Success metrics at one campus are directly transferable.


THREATS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

T1: GENDER RATIO FAILURE
    If the 50/50 ratio cannot be maintained, the entire product collapses.
    This is not a risk — it is an existential dependency. Every dating app
    in history has struggled with this. Yuni's strategies (waitlist,
    referral) are reasonable but unproven.

T2: LOW POOL SIZE AT LAUNCH
    The algorithm needs minimum ~50 users per gender per activity per
    time slot to form good groups. At launch, a single campus might have
    200 total users. With 10 activities and 5 time slots, the average
    pool per cell is 2 users. The algorithm will be useless; manual
    matching (by admins) will be required for months.

T3: ACTIVITY SUPPLY CONSTRAINT
    The app recommends "escape rooms, cooking classes, trivia" as Tier 1
    activities. But university towns may have limited supply of these
    venues, especially for weekly group bookings of 4-6 people. Venue
    availability may constrain the product more than the algorithm.

T4: COMPETITOR RESPONSE
    Hinge and Bumble have the resources to launch group features. If Yuni
    proves the model, larger competitors could replicate the concept with
    10x the engineering team and existing user base.

5. THE 5 CRITICAL CONDITIONS
================================================================================

These must be addressed before committing the full 24-week build.
They are not blockers — they are calibrations.

CONDITION 1: CORRECT THE CONVERSION TARGETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The research claims 30-45% conversion (second-date rate). The evidence
supports 15-25% as a defensible initial target. Use 20% as the baseline
for all planning and marketing.

WHY THIS MATTERS: If you build marketing around "1 in 3 dates leads to
a real date" and the actual number is 1 in 6, you have a credibility
crisis. If you build around "1 in 5" and deliver 1 in 4, you exceed
expectations. Under-promise, over-deliver.

ACTION: Update research.txt targets to "15-25% target, 30%+ stretch goal"
EFFORT: 10 minutes of text editing
RISK IF SKIPPED: Credibility damage with early users and investors


CONDITION 2: ADD A/B TEST GATES FOR UNPROVEN FEATURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Three features are built on inference rather than direct evidence:
(a) 4-point interest scale (vs. binary)
(b) Pre-date structured prompts (vs. single icebreaker)
(c) Soft match 48-hour mechanism (vs. no soft matches)

Each should be implemented with an A/B test from day one, not rolled
out as the default.

WHY THIS MATTERS: If the 4-point scale REDUCES match rates (users
selecting "Maybe" who would have said "Yes" in binary), you need to
detect this immediately and revert. Shipping these as permanent features
without measurement violates the scientific approach the project claims.

ACTION: Move Phase 7 (A/B testing) to Phase 1.5 — build the experiment
framework BEFORE building the features that need testing.
EFFORT: 2 additional weeks upfront, saves months of false assumptions
RISK IF SKIPPED: Building on unvalidated features for 6+ months


CONDITION 3: DEFINE THE COLD START LAUNCH STRATEGY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

No document addresses how Yuni acquires its first 200 users (100M + 100F)
at a single campus. The algorithm, the gender ratio management, the
conversion metrics — ALL depend on having a minimum viable pool.

The documents assume users exist and focus on optimizing their experience.
But user acquisition at a cold campus is a fundamentally different
problem that requires:
- Campus ambassador program (hire 5-10 students per campus)
- Launch event (a single curated group date with 40+ handpicked users)
- Social proof creation (photos, testimonials from launch event)
- Organic word-of-mouth from launch attendees

WHY THIS MATTERS: The best algorithm in the world is useless with 20
users. The first 3 months at any campus will be MANUAL matching by
admins (already in IDEA.md). The documentation should acknowledge this
phase explicitly and plan for it.

ACTION: Add a "Phase 0: Campus Launch Playbook" to PLAN.md
EFFORT: 1 week of planning, ongoing ops effort
RISK IF SKIPPED: Building a Ferrari with no road to drive it on


CONDITION 4: ADD FAILURE MODE ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The documentation describes the happy path in exhaustive detail but does
not analyze what happens when things go wrong:

- What if a campus has only 30 users? (Minimum viable pool)
- What if the gender ratio is 80/20 despite waitlist? (Ratio fails)
- What if women still report safety concerns in groups? (Edge case)
- What if the second-date bridge has a 5% acceptance rate? (Bridge fails)
- What if the algorithm's explore groups produce bad experiences? (Users
  assigned to 15% random groups have terrible dates)

Each failure mode needs a detection mechanism and a fallback strategy.

WHY THIS MATTERS: Startups rarely fail because the happy path doesn't
work. They fail because they didn't plan for the unhappy path.

ACTION: Add a "Failure Modes & Fallbacks" section to research.txt or
a new failure_modes.txt document
EFFORT: 1-2 days of analysis
RISK IF SKIPPED: First unexpected failure triggers panic instead of
planned response


CONDITION 5: SEQUENCE ANALYTICS BEFORE EVERYTHING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLAN.md puts analytics (Phase 1) before features (Phases 2-8). This is
correct. But STEPS.md and the implied build order suggest analytics is
"quick instrumentation" before the real work.

Analytics is not a checkbox. It is the FOUNDATION. Without it:
- You cannot measure baseline conversion (pre-redesign)
- You cannot detect if the 4-point scale helps or hurts
- You cannot verify gender ratio in real time
- You cannot know which activities actually convert
- You cannot learn algorithm weights

The analytics system must be COMPLETE and VERIFIED before ANY feature
is shipped to users. This means:
- Event logging for every user action
- Group outcome computation after every date
- Real-time gender ratio monitoring
- Conversion funnel visualization in admin dashboard
- A/B test framework with significance computation

WHY THIS MATTERS: If you ship features without measurement, you're
flying blind. You won't know what worked, what didn't, or why. This
is antithetical to the scientific approach that defines the project.

ACTION: Allocate 3 weeks (not 2) to Phase 1. Include A/B framework.
EFFORT: 1 additional week
RISK IF SKIPPED: 6 months of development with no data on impact

6. CORRECTED CONVERSION TARGETS
================================================================================

The research.txt claims "30-45% conversion rate" (users who attend a
group date and then meet someone from that group again romantically).

DECOMPOSITION:

    C = P(mutual_interest) x P(second_date | mutual_interest)

TERM 1: P(mutual_interest)

    Baseline: Speed dating = 60-87% get at least one mutual match
    But that's per-EVENT, not per-USER.

    Per-user in a group of 4 (2M + 2F):
    Each user has 2 cross-gender options.
    If per-pair probability = p, then:
    P(at least one mutual) = 1 - (1 - p^2)^2

    Speed dating per-pair probability: p ~ 0.25-0.35
    (From: avg 1.7 matches / 10 interactions = 0.17 match rate,
     but mutual = 0.17^2 overestimates; empirical mutual rate ~ 0.10)

    For Yuni (better conditions, attractiveness matching, longer time):
    Estimate p ~ 0.30-0.40

    P(mutual_interest) with p=0.30: 1 - (1-0.09)^2 = 0.17
    P(mutual_interest) with p=0.35: 1 - (1-0.12)^2 = 0.23
    P(mutual_interest) with p=0.40: 1 - (1-0.16)^2 = 0.29

    Including soft matches (add ~30% recovery of borderline):
    Adjusted: 0.22 - 0.38

    ESTIMATE: P(mutual_interest) = 25-40% per user per date

TERM 2: P(second_date | mutual_interest)

    Speed dating baseline: 6% go on a date / ~70% had a mutual match
    = P(follow_through) ~ 6/70 ~ 8.6%

    Yuni advantages over speed dating:
    - Already spent 2-3 hours together (vs. 5 minutes)
    - Have shared memories and inside jokes
    - Chat auto-opens in app (vs. exchanging numbers)
    - Second-date bridge actively suggests date

    Conservative multiplier: 3-5x improvement over speed dating
    P(second_date | mutual_interest) ~ 25-45%

COMBINED:

    C_low  = 0.25 x 0.25 = 6.3%   (pessimistic: conditions don't help)
    C_mid  = 0.32 x 0.35 = 11.2%  (moderate: some conditions help)
    C_high = 0.40 x 0.45 = 18.0%  (optimistic: most conditions help)

    │                                                                   │

    │ - Second-date bridge (build when you have real match data)       │
    │ - Soft matches (build when you have 4-point scale data)          │
    │ - Gender ratio automation (manual management at small scale)     │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

WHY THIS MVP ORDERING:

The single most important thing at launch is LEARNING VELOCITY.

You don't know if the 4-point scale helps. You don't know if values
matter. You don't know which activities convert. You don't know if
groups of 4 beat groups of 6.

The MVP gives you the INSTRUMENTS to answer these questions with real
data, while the admin team manually matches groups. The algorithm
redesign, second-date bridge, and advanced features are built on top
of REAL DATA, not assumptions.

This is how you build a science-driven company: measure first, then
optimize.
